The Intersections between the Internet, Geopolitics, and Digital Coloniality

In the contemporary world, the internet has evolved into more than a technological infrastructure; it has become a key arena where power, politics, and culture intersect and are contested. While originally framed as a decentralised and democratising force, the internet today reflects many of the global inequalities embedded in broader historical, political, and economic structures. In particular, the intersections between the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality have emerged as critical areas of analysis, revealing how digital technologies not only enable new forms of connection but also reproduce and entrench longstanding global asymmetries.

Geopolitical struggles increasingly play out through control over digital infrastructures, regulatory frameworks, and data governance. Powerful states and multinational corporations compete to shape the architecture of the internet, asserting influence over everything from undersea cables to international standards. Simultaneously, patterns of digital coloniality replicate colonial-era dynamics of domination and extraction: data flows from the Global South to the Global North, cultural content remains largely Western-centric, and epistemic hierarchies marginalise non-Western knowledges. As a result, many countries in the Global South experience a form of digital dependency, where their participation in the global internet is mediated through structures controlled by external powers.

This essay critically examines the intersections between the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality. Drawing on theoretical frameworks and recent developments, it explores how digital spaces have become arenas for geopolitical contestation while also perpetuating forms of colonial subordination in the digital age. In doing so, it highlights the urgent need to rethink digital governance, data sovereignty, and knowledge production in order to challenge these persistent inequalities.

The expansion of Meta’s 2Africa undersea cable network presents a powerful example of how the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality intersect in contemporary global affairs. Positioned as one of the most ambitious connectivity projects in history, 2Africa aims to encircle the African continent with over 45,000 kilometres of submarine cable, promising to significantly boost internet capacity, reduce latency, and lower prices for consumers (Meta, 2020). On the surface, such a development appears to offer tangible benefits for African economies, potentially narrowing the digital divide that has long marginalised much of the continent in the global information economy (World Bank, 2019).

Yet, when critically examined, the project reflects complex power dynamics that go far beyond infrastructure. While Meta and its partners frame the expansion as a philanthropic and developmental initiative, the reality is that control over digital infrastructure translates directly into political and economic power. Ownership of the cables allows corporations to control data routes, dictate terms of access, and extract significant value from the data flowing through these networks. In this way, digital infrastructure mirrors the colonial infrastructures of the past—railways, ports, and extraction routes—that were designed to facilitate the movement of resources to the benefit of external powers while offering limited local agency (Couldry and Mejías, 2019).

In the context of geopolitics, the 2Africa project must also be understood as part of the broader global contest over digital dominance. The United States and China have both recognised that controlling the physical and regulatory architecture of the internet yields strategic advantages, leading to fierce competition over who builds, owns, and regulates critical digital infrastructure (Feldstein, 2019). Africa, long positioned on the periphery of these global struggles, finds itself increasingly caught between these rival blocs, with its digital sovereignty at stake. The presence of companies like Meta in Africa’s connectivity landscape is not simply a matter of service provision; it embeds the continent into a global system where data and profits primarily circulate back to corporate headquarters in Silicon Valley, not to African institutions (Mothobi and Gillwald, 2019).

This raises profound concerns about data sovereignty and digital dependency. Despite the influx of foreign investment into African telecommunications, most of the platforms that store, process, and monetise African users’ data are based in the Global North. This dynamic risks reducing African states to mere sources of raw data—a digital equivalent of the raw material extraction that defined earlier colonial economies. As Graham (2014) observes, digital geographies often reproduce the same uneven patterns seen in earlier global economic structures, where the benefits of technological integration are unequally distributed. The Global North maintains its role as the principal architect and beneficiary of digital systems, while the Global South occupies a structurally dependent position.

However, the issue extends beyond economics into the realm of epistemic control. Control over digital infrastructure also means control over cultural representation, knowledge production, and global discourse. As Graham (2014) notes, much of the digital content that circulates globally is produced in a narrow range of languages and cultural contexts, often marginalising non-Western knowledge systems and worldviews. Even as access to the internet expands, the risk is that African voices remain peripheral, not due to lack of connectivity but because the platforms themselves favour certain types of content and cultural norms that reflect the interests and values of their owners (Birhane, 2020).

The implications of this are significant. If African states and societies do not assert stronger governance over their digital futures, the expansion of connectivity may deepen existing inequalities rather than resolve them. Infrastructure alone is insufficient if it is accompanied by continued external control over data, algorithms, platforms, and cultural narratives. As Couldry and Mejías (2019) argue, digital coloniality operates through the extraction not only of economic resources but also of social and cultural value, shaping how individuals understand themselves and their place in the world.

At the same time, some African institutions and regional bodies are becoming more attuned to these challenges. The African Union’s Digital Transformation Strategy and emerging data governance frameworks seek to reclaim a measure of sovereignty over the continent’s digital development (African Union, 2020). Yet, these initiatives remain in early stages, often facing powerful resistance from entrenched corporate interests and the complexities of negotiating with foreign investors who control much of the capital and technical expertise needed to build digital infrastructure.

Beyond individual case studies, the intersection of the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality reflects broader systemic dynamics that shape the contemporary global order. To fully grasp these intersections, it is necessary to move beyond infrastructural concerns and explore how political, economic, and epistemic power operate simultaneously through digital technologies.

At its core, the internet represents both a site and an instrument of geopolitical power. States have increasingly recognised that control over digital infrastructure, data flows, and governance frameworks equates to new forms of strategic influence. This has given rise to what some scholars describe as cyber-geopolitics — the use of digital technologies as tools for advancing national interests, projecting power, and safeguarding sovereignty (Maurer, 2018). For dominant powers, securing access to global digital infrastructures allows for expansive surveillance capacities, economic advantage, and the ability to shape global narratives. For weaker states, particularly those in the Global South, participation in the digital economy often comes at the cost of surrendering control to powerful multinational corporations or foreign states that own the platforms, cables, data centres, and regulatory standards upon which their digital economies depend (Feldstein, 2019).

Digital coloniality builds on this geopolitical asymmetry by extending the logic of colonial domination into the digital sphere. As Couldry and Mejías (2019) argue, data colonialism operates through the appropriation of human life and social activity, transforming them into sources of profit for data capitalists primarily based in the Global North. Unlike earlier forms of colonialism that extracted physical resources, digital colonialism extracts informational and cognitive resources, commodifying personal data, social interactions, cultural production, and even intimate aspects of identity (Thatcher et al., 2016). This extraction is not merely an economic transaction but a reconfiguration of power that affects how knowledge is produced, how subjects are governed, and how futures are imagined.

Crucially, digital coloniality operates not only through corporate extraction but also through normative and epistemic dominance. Western technological systems embed particular cultural values and assumptions into algorithms, platforms, and protocols. As Graham (2014) notes, most of the world’s digital content is produced and curated in a narrow set of languages, privileging Western worldviews while marginalising alternative epistemologies. Even when the internet expands to previously disconnected populations, it does so largely on terms defined by Western corporate and state interests, thus reasserting epistemic hierarchies that echo colonial patterns of knowledge exclusion (Birhane, 2020).

The governance of the internet itself reflects these dynamics of exclusion. Global internet governance institutions, such as ICANN and the Internet Governance Forum, remain dominated by actors from the Global North, while voices from the Global South struggle to assert meaningful influence over key decisions (Kleinwächter, 2016). This governance imbalance reinforces digital dependency, as developing countries must navigate rules and frameworks that are shaped by the interests of others. Even efforts to establish alternative governance models — such as China’s promotion of digital sovereignty and state-led internet models — risk replacing one form of digital domination with another rather than empowering genuinely autonomous local governance (Deibert, 2015).

At the intersection of geopolitics and digital coloniality, we thus witness a convergence of control over both infrastructure and meaning. Physical networks enable the movement of data across borders, but equally significant are the power structures that determine how data is used, who benefits from it, and whose knowledge is legitimised. As digital connectivity expands, so too does the risk of entrenching global hierarchies unless concerted efforts are made to build alternative infrastructures, diversify epistemic centres, and assert the right of digital self-determination for all nations and communities.

In this context, calls for digital decolonisation are increasingly urgent. Scholars and activists have begun advocating for policies that promote data sovereignty, foster local content creation, support open-source technologies, and strengthen regional cooperation to challenge digital dependency (Ndemo and Weiss, 2017). Without such interventions, the promise of the internet as a democratising force may remain hollow, serving instead as a new frontier for 21st-century forms of domination that replicate and extend the legacies of historical colonialism.

The intersections between the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality reveal the complex entanglement of power, technology, and historical structures in the contemporary digital landscape. While the internet was once heralded as a democratising force with the potential to empower previously marginalised voices, its global expansion has largely reinforced longstanding inequalities shaped by colonial histories and geopolitical rivalries. As the case of Meta’s 2Africa cable demonstrates, even projects framed as developmental interventions can perpetuate digital dependency, with ownership and control remaining concentrated in the hands of powerful corporations and states largely situated in the Global North.

Beyond infrastructure, digital coloniality operates through subtler yet equally consequential mechanisms of epistemic domination, data extraction, and governance exclusion. Control over data flows, content curation, and platform algorithms privileges particular worldviews, languages, and cultures while marginalising others. Meanwhile, geopolitical contests over digital infrastructure and regulatory frameworks reflect the increasingly strategic role that digital technologies play in global power dynamics, leaving many countries of the Global South navigating an international system that continues to limit their sovereignty and agency.

Confronting these challenges requires moving beyond narrow developmentalist narratives of connectivity to critically examine who owns, controls, and benefits from digital infrastructures and knowledge systems. Efforts towards digital decolonisation — including data sovereignty, regional cooperation, and support for indigenous knowledge systems — offer promising pathways for challenging these persistent structures of domination. However, such transformations demand sustained political will, institutional reform, and global solidarity to ensure that the internet becomes a genuinely inclusive space that serves the diverse interests of all peoples, rather than reproducing the hierarchies of the past in new digital forms.
