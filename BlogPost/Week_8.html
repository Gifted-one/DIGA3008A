Blog 8

April 21, 2025

Digital Divide Widens

Tataki and Glynos’ blog, Digital Divide Widens, offers a compelling overview of how the COVID-19 pandemic deepened global digital inequalities, disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations. Over 1.5 billion students were impacted by school closures, yet half lacked access to a computer and 40% had no internet at home—exclusion most severe in low-income regions like Sub-Saharan Africa and among girls worldwide. In the workforce, only those in high-income, tech-based roles could transition to remote work, while low-paid and less-educated workers faced greater risks of unemployment, unable to telework.

The blog powerfully illustrates how access to technology became a lifeline for education and employment during lockdowns. However, it is not just about having access, but also about the quality of that access, digital literacy, and the ability to use digital tools effectively. For many low-income families, owning a mobile phone does not equate to meaningful digital engagement, especially when online education requires stable connectivity or parental involvement. The digital gender gap, while acknowledged, deserves deeper exploration; in many communities, cultural norms and economic constraints make access even more difficult for girls unless gender-sensitive interventions are implemented.

Temporary measures like lending devices or offering free data were helpful during the crisis but remain insufficient. The authors rightly argue that equitable access to digital infrastructure must become a lasting priority. Systemic solutions are needed: universal broadband access, particularly in rural and underserved areas; digital community hubs in libraries and schools; subsidies for devices and repairs; and culturally relevant digital literacy programmes. Long-term success also depends on inclusive policymaking that involves those most affected—youth, women, people with disabilities—to ensure solutions are relevant and equitable.

Importantly, the blog frames internet access as a basic human right—a perspective that aligns with global development goals. Governments must treat digital inclusion as essential social infrastructure, not a luxury. Only through inclusive, sustained efforts can we ensure the internet becomes a tool for empowerment, not exclusion.

Internet Access is a fundamental right

Claire Daniolou’s blog, “The Need for Global Internet Connectivity,” presents a persuasive case for the expansion of internet access, especially in rural and underserved areas. While the blog effectively outlines the numerous social, educational, and economic benefits of broadband access—particularly in times of crisis like the COVID-19 pandemic—it also acknowledges a critical, often underexplored aspect of digital inclusion: the potential dangers of internet misuse and the inadequacy of access alone without digital literacy.

Daniolou rightly asserts that internet access is no longer a luxury, but a necessity for participation in modern society. However, she also recognizes that the internet is not inherently benevolent. One of the blog's strengths lies in its warning that, like any powerful tool, the internet can do harm if misused. Misinformation, manipulation, and online radicalization are real threats, particularly in environments where users lack the skills to critically evaluate digital content. These dangers are exacerbated by algorithm-driven echo chambers and the spread of disinformation, which can deepen social divisions, undermine democratic processes, and promote harmful ideologies.

Furthermore, Daniolou touches on the risk of cultural erosion—where global internet culture, dominated by a few powerful platforms, can suppress local identities, languages, and traditions. While access to global networks can foster diversity and inclusion, without deliberate efforts to protect and promote local culture, digital connectivity may inadvertently contribute to cultural homogenization.

The blog makes a crucial observation: access alone is not enough. Simply connecting people to the internet without equipping them with the knowledge and skills to navigate it responsibly can be counterproductive. In digitally naive communities, users may fall prey to scams, surveillance, or exploitative online practices. Therefore, any push for broadband expansion must be paired with comprehensive digital education—especially in critical thinking, data privacy, media literacy, and online safety.

Although Daniolou briefly mentions the importance of technological education, the blog could have engaged more deeply with how such education can be effectively implemented. Questions remain: Who will deliver digital literacy programmes? How can these be culturally adapted and made accessible across diverse populations? How can vulnerable groups—such as the elderly, women in patriarchal societies, and people with disabilities—be included in these efforts?

In summary, Daniolou provides a well-rounded introduction to the necessity of global internet connectivity, but her most important insight is that inclusion cannot stop at access. Without adequate safeguards, critical skills, and culturally sensitive education, expanding connectivity may replicate or even worsen existing inequalities. True digital inclusion requires not just infrastructure, but empowerment—and this demands long-term, participatory strategies that treat users not just as consumers of the internet, but as informed and capable digital citizens.
