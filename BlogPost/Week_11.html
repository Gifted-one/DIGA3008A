Decolonizing code

Decolonialism in the context of coding demands more than awareness—it calls for an active dismantling of embedded power structures that shape how software is imagined, built, and deployed. At its core, coding is never neutral. The assumptions that underpin programming languages, development tools, and design standards are deeply informed by Western epistemologies. The prioritisation of efficiency, abstraction, and modularity may be seen as universal virtues in programming, but they arise from Enlightenment-era logic and Cartesian dualism. This worldview privileges rationality, linear progress, and objectivity—values that often marginalise alternative ways of knowing, particularly those rooted in Indigenous, communal, or spiritual traditions. Thus, a decolonial lens encourages coders to question the supremacy of these paradigms and explore how pluralistic, relational, or cyclical knowledge systems might reshape what we think “good code” looks like.

Digital infrastructure itself also reproduces colonial dynamics. Most of the world’s internet infrastructure—cloud platforms, DNS systems, server farms—is controlled by a small number of corporations in the Global North. When developers uncritically build on services like Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, or GitHub, they risk perpetuating dependencies that mirror colonial economic relations: centralised control, resource extraction, and asymmetrical benefit. In this way, the notion of digital sovereignty becomes central to a decolonial coding ethic. Developers must ask: whose data are we collecting, where is it stored, and who profits from it? Supporting decentralised architectures, open standards, and locally governed infrastructure can be meaningful steps toward resisting digital imperialism.

Furthermore, the way we treat data mirrors colonial patterns of extraction. Just as colonial regimes plundered physical resources, today’s algorithms harvest behavioural data, linguistic patterns, and biometric information—often from marginalised communities—without consent or compensation. This is evident in facial recognition technologies trained on datasets collected from people in the Global South, or Indigenous languages scraped from online repositories to train AI systems controlled by Western firms. Decolonising code, therefore, involves building systems that centre consent, reciprocity, and data justice. It requires treating data not as raw material to be mined, but as cultural property embedded in social and historical context.

Even open-source communities, often viewed as egalitarian and inclusive, are not immune from critique. Core contributors and maintainers are predominantly white, male, and Western, and participation often assumes fluency in English, familiarity with Git workflows, and a capacity to work without pay—assumptions that can exclude developers from underrepresented regions. A decolonial approach to open source demands a rethinking of contribution models to centre inclusion, mentorship, and flexibility. Projects should consider translating documentation, restructuring governance to reflect regional leadership, and creating spaces that honour local contexts rather than enforcing global homogeneity.

The dominance of English in programming—both in syntax and documentation—also merits scrutiny. Language is not merely a medium of instruction; it shapes thought. Concepts like “variable,” “function,” or “object” encode particular worldviews. Programming interfaces, too, often assume left-to-right text flow, alphabetic ordering, and a Western visual logic that excludes those from oral cultures or non-Latin scripts. A decolonial practice of code would involve challenging the linguistic hegemony of English, promoting the development of programming languages and environments that reflect other epistemologies, and designing for linguistic and symbolic diversity.

Our treatment of time within code further reflects colonial assumptions. Most digital systems operate according to the Gregorian calendar, UTC time zones, and linear conceptions of progress. Yet many Indigenous and non-Western cultures perceive time cyclically, seasonally, or relationally. When we hard-code Western time structures into our systems, we flatten and erase these alternative temporalities. A decolonial approach to time in code could involve designing for plural temporal frameworks, respecting local calendars, or allowing for spiritual and ceremonial timing in systems used by Indigenous communities.

User interface and experience (UI/UX) design standards also privilege Western aesthetics—minimalist layouts, greyscale colour palettes, “uncluttered” white space—all of which stem from Euro-American design traditions like Bauhaus. Navigation metaphors like “folders” or “desktops” assume familiarity with Western bureaucratic systems. These may alienate users whose cognitive and cultural schemas do not align with these conventions. A decolonial UI/UX approach would begin with participatory design: involving communities not as test subjects but as co-creators of the visual and interactional grammar of their tools. This not only increases usability but affirms cultural identity through design.

Even licensing regimes carry colonial baggage. Open-source licenses like MIT or GPL assume Western legal frameworks based on individual authorship and property. This runs counter to Indigenous and collectivist understandings of knowledge as communal, sacred, or intergenerational. Some communities may not wish for their knowledge to be “freely available” in the Western legal sense. Developers engaged in decolonial practice should explore or adopt alternative licensing models—such as Traditional Knowledge (TK) labels or community-use agreements—that prioritise ethical use, attribution, and non-exploitation.

Labour inequality is another essential dimension. Much of the world’s codebase is written by underpaid developers in outsourced environments—particularly in countries historically colonised and economically disadvantaged. These workers often lack creative autonomy, working invisibly behind Western-facing products. A decolonial practice means advocating for fair pay, equitable working conditions, and shifting away from viewing Global South developers as disposable “cheap labour.” It also involves resisting the startup culture’s fetish for speed and disruption, which often relies on burning out underpaid coders and ignoring long-term social impact.

Lastly, decolonialism urges us to confront the technosolutionist mindset that permeates programming culture. There’s a tendency to treat code as a fix for every social issue—without engaging communities or understanding historical context. This mirrors colonial paternalism: assuming that others need saving, and that external (usually Western) intervention is inherently beneficial. A decolonial approach calls for humility. It means resisting the urge to always build, and instead taking the time to listen, reflect, and collaborate with those directly affected by the technology.

In summary, a decolonial coding practice is not a matter of adding diversity slogans or checking boxes. It requires deep structural transformation—rethinking how knowledge is produced, how power circulates, and who gets to decide what technologies are built, how, and for whom. It challenges us to unlearn dominance, question neutrality, and code with care, accountability, and solidarity. True decolonisation in tech is not metaphorical—it is political, and it begins with the code we write and the systems we choose to support.
