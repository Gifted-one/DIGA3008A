Social capital and the three levels of digital divide

Ruiu and Ragnedda's article presents a critical examination of digital inequalities through the lens of social capital theory, arguing that the digital divide operates across three interconnected levels: access (first level), skills and usage (second level), and outcomes (third level). Their framework demonstrates how pre-existing social, economic, and cultural capital shapes individuals' digital engagement and their ability to convert online activities into tangible offline benefits. By applying Bourdieu's theory of capital to digital spaces, the authors reveal how digital capital functions as both a product and reinforcer of social stratification, with privileged groups leveraging their offline advantages to gain further benefits online, while marginalized groups face compounded exclusion.

The authors develop their argument through five key mechanisms where social and digital capital intersect: virtual communities (strong ties), weak professional ties, network expansion, online visibility, and trust in digital environments. Each mechanism illustrates how the internet reproduces rather than disrupts existing inequalities, as those with greater offline capital are better positioned to develop digital capital and use it advantageously. This analysis challenges techno-optimistic narratives about digital democratization, instead positioning digital inequality as an extension of systemic social hierarchies that requires structural, rather than merely technical, solutions.

“Social, economic and cultural capital not only generate digital divide between people who can and cannot access the Internet (first level of digital divide), but also inequalities in terms of motivation, skills and purpose of use (second level of digital divide) and inequalities in terms of (social/cultural/economic/personal/political) benefits they can gain on-line (third level of digital divide).”

This quote outlines the authors' conceptualization of the digital divide as a layered issue rooted in broader social inequalities. It reinforces that digital access is not just about owning a device or having an internet connection (first level), but also involves the competencies to use technology meaningfully (second level), and the ability to derive tangible value from it (third level). The framework here aligns with Bourdieu’s theory of capital, particularly his assertion that different forms of capital reproduce social structures. In this case, those with more economic, social, and cultural capital offline are better positioned to access and benefit from digital tools, thereby deepening existing inequalities. The authors reject a techno-utopian view and instead emphasize a structuralist interpretation of digital engagement, where power and privilege mediate participation.

“Digital capital has the power to (re)produce inequalities, perpetuating processes of digital inclusion and exclusion and influencing one’s position in the social structure.”

Here, the authors suggest that digital capital operates not just as a neutral resource but as a mechanism of social reproduction. The phrase “(re)produce inequalities” explicitly draws attention to how digital technologies can entrench existing disparities if left unaddressed. From a critical theory standpoint, this supports the notion that digital spaces are not inherently equitable; they are shaped by the same structural constraints that govern offline life. This idea resonates with Bourdieu's field theory, where individuals bring their capital into a field (in this case, the digital field), and their relative positions and possibilities are influenced by what they possess. Rather than levelling the playing field, digital capital often reflects and magnifies offline privilege, creating “digital elites” and further marginalizing disadvantaged groups.

“People already endowed with a certain amount of capital in the offline environment tend to reproduce it online, strengthening their existing resources.”

This quote supports the argument that digital inequality is not a new phenomenon but a continuation of long-standing social hierarchies in a different form. The internet, rather than acting as a space of democratization, becomes a platform for capital reinforcement. Those who have educational, economic, and social resources are more likely to exploit digital tools effectively, expanding their networks, knowledge, and opportunities. This insight corresponds directly with Bourdieu’s concept of “capital conversion,” where one form of capital (e.g. cultural) can be transformed into another (e.g. social or economic), thereby securing and reinforcing one's dominant position. Ruiu and Ragnedda thus make the case that policy efforts to reduce the digital divide must go beyond providing access—they must address the underlying inequalities that determine how digital tools are used.

“Digital capital is not only an instrument to access information, but also a mechanism that affects individuals’ positions within the social hierarchy.”

In this quote, the authors summarize a key point: that digital capital is not passive or purely technical—it is deeply social and political. It contributes to shaping social trajectories, granting some individuals and groups greater visibility, voice, and opportunity, while others remain digitally (and socially) invisible. From a critical media studies perspective, this statement challenges narratives that view technology as neutral. It calls for a more intersectional and justice-oriented analysis of digital participation, one that includes race, class, education, and geography as determining factors. Digital capital, like any form of power, can either liberate or exclude—depending on the broader socio-political context in which it operates.

“It is important to understand how social inequalities are reproduced online and what forms of capital are necessary to transform online activities into real benefits.”

This quote directly engages with the question of how structural inequalities are replicated in digital spaces. It implies that access alone does not guarantee benefit—individuals must possess certain types of capital (e.g. digital literacy, time, motivation) to convert online engagement into offline advantages. The authors are therefore applying a Bourdieusian framework of capital conversion, highlighting that digital participation is not uniformly productive. Those lacking in cultural and social capital may engage online but fail to achieve the same gains as those from more privileged backgrounds. This understanding calls for more nuanced digital inclusion policies that not only provide tools but also build capacity for effective, transformative use.

“Social capital is not equally distributed among the population, and neither is digital capital. This leads to a situation in which digital resources are unequally used and unequally beneficial.”

Here, the authors highlight the interdependence between offline and online forms of capital. Their assertion points to the systemic nature of inequality—social structures predetermine who gains the most from digital engagement. In this sense, digital capital, like social capital, becomes a mechanism of stratification. This quote supports a critical sociology of technology, one that resists technological determinism and instead sees digital participation as deeply embedded in pre-existing class, educational, and cultural hierarchies. From a critical realist viewpoint, it also suggests that digital inequality is symptomatic of broader societal issues and must be addressed through systemic social reform rather than solely through technological fixes.

“The potential of the internet to generate social capital is higher for those who already have it.”

This statement reinforces the cumulative advantage hypothesis: the internet amplifies benefits for those already in a position of strength. The authors underline how the digital realm mirrors the "Matthew Effect" (Merton, 1968)—the idea that the rich get richer. In this context, social capital acts as a multiplier; people with broad offline networks, high education, and confidence in navigating systems are better able to build and maintain digital relationships that offer further opportunities. Those without these advantages may use the internet but do so in less socially or economically fruitful ways. Thus, the internet's capacity to generate social capital is unevenly realized.

“The digital divide is not a single gap to be bridged but a complex and multi-layered phenomenon that mirrors existing social structures.”

This quote encapsulates the central argument of the article—that the digital divide is not just about physical access, but about layers of inequality tied to social status, education, income, and cultural practices. The authors reject simplistic binary understandings of "connected vs unconnected" and instead advocate for a stratified model, which aligns with Bourdieu’s theory of social space where various capitals interact to produce class distinctions. From a critical framework, this view challenges policymakers to address root causes of inequality rather than implementing surface-level interventions such as device distribution alone.

The preceding analysis of key quotes from Ruiu and Ragnedda's work collectively underscores their central thesis: digital inequalities are not standalone technological issues but manifestations of deeper social stratification. Their Bourdieusian framework reveals how digital capital operates as a mechanism of social reproduction, with offline privileges being converted into online advantages through various forms of capital interchange. This perspective has significant implications for digital inclusion policies, suggesting they must address root causes of inequality rather than focusing solely on access provision. Ultimately, the authors provide a powerful theoretical lens for understanding digital participation as embedded within—and shaped by—existing power structures and social hierarchies. Their work makes a compelling case for approaching digital inequality as a complex, multi-layered phenomenon that demands intersectional analysis and systemic interventions.

References

1. Ruiu, M.L. and Ragnedda, M., 2017. Social capital and the three levels of digital divide. In: M. Ragnedda and G.W. Muschert, eds. Theorizing Digital Divides. Abingdon: Routledge, pp.21–34.

2. Merton, R.K., 1968. The Matthew Effect in Science. Science, 159(3810), pp.56–63.
