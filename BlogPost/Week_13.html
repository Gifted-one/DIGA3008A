How I would approach justice and ethics in your artistic and programming practice

Justice and Ethics in My Artistic and Programming Practice

As I continue to develop my personal website and expand my understanding of both visual design and programming, I find myself increasingly drawn to the ethical dimensions of what I create. I’ve come to believe that technical and aesthetic choices are never neutral, they reflect values, priorities, and power. Whether I’m working on navigation features, styling elements with CSS, or implementing JavaScript interactions, I’m making decisions that shape the experience of others. This is why I believe it's essential to embed a sense of justice and ethical responsibility into my artistic and coding practice.

Designing with Respect for Users

From the beginning, I’ve tried to be intentional about how users interact with my site. One of the key features I implemented is a carousel navigation component that displays three cards at a time. The middle card is dominant, and users can navigate left or right without being trapped in an infinite loop. I designed it this way because I want users to feel in control, not manipulated. I’ve seen many interfaces that disorient users with endless scrolling or unclear options, and I think that kind of design erodes trust. I want my site to provide clarity and structure, a space that users can navigate with ease.

Another example of this is the reading progress tracker I built. As users scroll through a page, a bar and percentage update to reflect how much they’ve read. This information isn’t hidden or abstract, it’s presented transparently. I believe in making reading experiences feel engaging, but not gamified. This tool isn't meant to pressure users to finish quickly or consume more, but rather to support reflection. It tells users: “Here’s how far you’ve come,” and gives them a way to monitor their own pace.

Supporting Navigation and Orientation

Long-form content can often be disorienting. To address this, I added a sidebar that highlights which section the reader is currently viewing. I think of this as a way to respect the user's cognitive load. Rather than forcing someone to guess where they are on a page, the site tells them clearly. It provides orientation — like a digital signpost.

This is where I see the link between usability and ethics. Ethical design is not only about avoiding harm, but also about making the experience thoughtful and supportive. When a website honors the user’s time and effort by making content easy to explore, it promotes a more just relationship between creator and audience.

Aesthetic Expression as Ethical Resistance

Aesthetically, I’ve chosen to work with bold, playful fonts like Dynapuff, strong black outlines, and a vibrant color palette. These decisions give my website personality, and I think that’s important. Too often, websites adopt cold, corporate minimalism that prioritizes performance metrics over expression. In contrast, I want my site to feel personal, human, and creative.

This, too, is an ethical choice. I’m resisting the standardization of the web. I’m creating a space that reflects individuality and care. I think this matters because design communicates who we think we’re designing for. If my site looks and feels like it was made by a person, not a brand — then users may feel more welcomed, more seen, and more inclined to engage sincerely.

Rejecting Manipulation and Embracing Transparency

One of the most important values I bring to both my programming and design work is a rejection of dark patterns, the manipulative interface tricks that trick people into doing things they didn’t intend. I’ve seen these tactics used in cookie consent popups, email subscriptions, and checkout flows. I believe they undermine the web as a space of trust.

On my site, I avoid pre-checked boxes, deceptive buttons, and obscure settings. If I ever request user input or data, it will be clear, optional, and respectful. While I currently don’t collect user data, I still think carefully about how I would handle privacy and consent if I did. I believe users should always know what’s being collected, why, and how to opt out.

Tracking Progress with Purpose

Another feature I implemented is a dashboard that tracks how much of each blog post or subpage a user has read. This is stored using local storage per page, so progress can persist between sessions and update only when users scroll further. The dashboard gives users a clear sense of where they’ve been, and helps them revisit unfinished content.

What I find powerful about this feature is that it’s user-centered, not extractive. I’m not collecting data to optimize engagement or increase ad revenue, I’m offering a reflective tool. It supports memory, self-directed learning, and autonomy. I believe that’s what ethical digital tools should do.

Justice and Care as Creative Materials

To me, justice in digital design isn’t just about accessibility compliance or clean code. It’s about how I treat users — through layout, language, interaction, and aesthetic decisions. It’s about how I build systems that value users’ time, intelligence, and agency.

That’s why I design interfaces that are clear and respectful, avoid manipulation, and celebrate personality. I want my site to be a space of care, not coercion. And I want the skills I’m building, in both art and programming, to reflect that. Justice and ethics aren’t afterthoughts; they’re part of the creative process itself.
